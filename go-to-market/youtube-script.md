**[1:30 – 4:30 — THE 5 GAPS]**

*[Screen share: simple text slides for each point]*

**Gap 1: ACV instead of replacement cost**

"Look at your policy's valuation section — usually Section 4. Search for 'replacement cost' or 'RC.' If your policy says replacement cost and your settlement used actual cash value — meaning they applied depreciation — that's disputable.

On a $40,000 claim, this difference alone can be $8,000 to $12,000."

**Gap 2: Business income calculated wrong**

"If your policy includes business interruption coverage and your insurer calculated your lost income as a flat daily rate — they probably did it wrong.

Most commercial property policies say business income loss is calculated based on a trailing 3-month or 12-month average. Not a flat daily estimate. Check your Section 5.

The difference: on a 30-day closure, a flat-rate calculation might give you $6,000. The trailing average calculation might give you $14,000."

**Gap 3: Smoke remediation omitted**

"For fire claims specifically — smoke and soot remediation is where adjusters cut the most. Standard scope for a commercial smoke loss includes: HVAC duct cleaning, soft goods deodorization, air quality testing, and contents restoration.

If your estimate doesn't include these line items, cite them by name and request a revised scope."

**Gap 4: Code upgrade costs missing**

"When you rebuild after a covered loss, local code may require upgrades. This can be significant — new electrical panels, updated sprinkler systems, ADA compliance work.

Check your declarations page for 'Ordinance or Law' or 'Code Upgrade' coverage. If it's listed and your estimate doesn't include it, that's a dispute point."

**Gap 5: The itemization was never requested**

"The simplest gap of all: most business owners never ask for the line-item breakdown of how their settlement was calculated.

You are legally entitled to this. Call your adjuster and say: 'Please provide me with a written, itemized breakdown of the claim calculation, including all line items included and excluded, and the specific policy provisions cited for any reduction.'

Once you have it — compare every line against your own estimates."

---

**[4:30 – 6:00 — HOW TO DISPUTE]**

"Once you've identified the gap, the dispute letter is three paragraphs:

Paragraph 1: What you received. 'On [date] we received a settlement offer of $X.'

Paragraph 2: What you believe is correct. 'Based on our independent contractor estimates and the policy provisions below, we believe the fair settlement is $Y.'

Paragraph 3: Cite each clause specifically. Section number, quoted language, how it applies.

That's it. Keep it factual. No emotional language. You're citing a contract, not arguing.

Send it certified mail. State that you expect a written response within 30 days. In most states, they're legally required to respond."
