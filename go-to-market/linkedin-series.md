## Post 1 — The $14,200 hook

The average commercial insurance settlement offer is $14,200 below what the policy actually requires.

Most small business owners never find out.

Here's how insurers do it:

1. They calculate using ACV (actual cash value) when your policy says replacement cost. Difference on a $40k claim: $8,000–$12,000.

2. They use a flat daily rate for business income instead of the trailing 3-month average your policy specifies. Difference on a 30-day closure: $5,000–$8,000.

3. They omit smoke remediation line items that are standard scope. Difference: $2,000–$6,000.

4. They exclude ordinance/law (code upgrade) costs even when you have the endorsement. Difference: $1,000–$15,000 depending on age of building.

You don't need an attorney to dispute this. You need the policy language — cited correctly.

## Post 2 — The one email that recovers thousands

Sent this email to your adjuster. Costs nothing. Takes 30 seconds.

---
"Please provide me with a written, itemized breakdown of the claim payment calculation, including all line items included and excluded, and the specific policy provisions cited for any reduction or denial."
---

This is a legal right in most states — not a favor.

Once you have the itemization, compare each line against:
→ Your own contractor estimates
→ Your actual policy language
→ Standard scope for your incident type

Most people never ask for it. Most people who ask find something.

## Post 3 — Story post (restaurant fire example)

A restaurant owner in Asheville NC received a $28,400 settlement after a kitchen fire.

She almost signed it.

Before she did, she found three things in her policy:

1. §4.2: Replacement cost, not ACV. The adjuster used ACV on $14k of commercial kitchen equipment.

2. §3.4: Smoke remediation scope includes HVAC duct cleaning. Not in the estimate.

3. §5.1: Business income loss = trailing 3-month average. The adjuster used a flat daily rate.

She sent a one-page letter citing each of these. Six weeks later: $46,200.

No attorney. No public adjuster. One letter.

The policy is a contract. You can cite it back.

## Post 4 — State law fact

Most small business owners don't know this:

In [X] states, if your insurer unreasonably delays or denies a valid claim, you can sue for:
→ The original claim amount
→ PLUS extra damages (often 2x or 3x)
→ PLUS attorney's fees

This is called "bad faith" liability.

Examples:
- Colorado: 2x damages for unreasonable delay
- Washington: Triple damages under the Insurance Fair Conduct Act
- Tennessee: 25% penalty + attorney fees for bad faith refusal
- Pennsylvania: Punitive damages, interest, attorney fees

If your insurer has been stalling, lowballing, or sending vague denials — check your state's bad faith law. Time limits vary: Louisiana is 1 year from date of loss. Kentucky is 15 years.

## Post 5 — Practical tip

Before you accept any commercial insurance settlement:

1. Request the itemized breakdown (they're required to provide it)
2. Read Section 4 (Valuation) of your policy
3. Read Section 5 (Business Income) if you closed during repairs
4. Read your declarations page for endorsements
5. Get one independent contractor estimate

You are not required to accept the first offer.
You are not required to accept any offer within the policy period.
You have the right to dispute in writing citing specific policy language.

## Post 6 — Common misconception

"I don't want to make my insurer angry by disputing."

This is the most expensive belief a small business owner can have.

Insurance adjusters are trained to close claims quickly at the lowest possible number. A dispute letter citing specific policy clauses is not a personal attack — it is exactly how the process is designed to work.

You're not asking for a favor. You're enforcing a contract.

The insurer already collected your premium. They know the policy language. They're counting on the fact that you don't.

## Post 7 — The proof of loss trap

Most business owners don't know this deadline exists.

After a covered loss, your policy requires you to file a "sworn proof of loss" — a formal, signed statement of your claim — within a specific window.

Common timeframes: 30 days, 60 days, 90 days.

Miss it and the insurer can use it to contest your claim entirely.

What to do:
→ Read your policy for "proof of loss" or "duties after loss"
→ File within the specified window even if you're still documenting
→ Note: you can file an amended proof of loss later if your damages increase
→ Send certified mail and keep the return receipt

## Post 8 — The appraisal clause

If you and your insurer can't agree on the settlement amount, there's a mechanism built into most commercial policies: the appraisal clause.

How it works:
→ You hire your own independent appraiser
→ The insurer hires their own
→ The two appraisers agree on a neutral umpire
→ Any two of the three agree on a number → that number is binding

No attorneys required. Faster than litigation. Usually 30–90 days.

Most policyholders don't know this exists. Most insurers don't mention it.

If you're stuck in a dispute, invoking the appraisal clause is often the fastest path to a fair resolution.

## Post 9 — The silent endorsement

The most valuable coverage in your commercial policy is probably one you don't know you have.

Check your declarations page for any of these:
- Ordinance or Law (pays for code-required upgrades during a rebuild)
- Equipment Breakdown (covers sudden mechanical failure — standard property doesn't)
- Sewer Backup (standard property explicitly excludes it)
- Utility Service Interruption (covers power outage losses)
- Contingent Business Interruption (covers losses from a supplier's disaster)

If any of these appear on your declarations and aren't in your settlement — that's a dispute point.
